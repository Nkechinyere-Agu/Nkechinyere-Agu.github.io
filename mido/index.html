Enabling Trust in Medical Imaging

Nkechinyere N. Agu1, Ismini Lorenzou2, Mehdi Moradi3, Pingkun Yan1, James Hendler1,
1Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute | 2Virginia Tech, Blacksburg | 3Google, Mountain View, CA |

A website to navigate resources open-sourced for the Medical Imaging and Diagnostic Ontology. Use the side navigation panel to explore different sections of the website.

Abstract

Chest X-Ray (CXR) imaging is the most common form of medical imaging used in clinical processes. Typical CXR interpretation involves several stages of reasoning and careful analysis from the radiologist before reaching a final assessment. Most deep learning frameworks utilize only the imaging data in its entirety to reach a clinical decision, ignoring important anatomical information and existing domain knowledge. In this work, we introduce an explainable AI reasoning system that integrates anatomical visual information, patient history information and external domain knowledge while making decisions. In particular, we propose the use of semantic web technologies, such as ontologies and knowledge graphs, to bridge the gap in existing deep learning systems, enable domain-guided reasoning capabilities and enhance model trustworthiness. We conduct in-depth evaluation of our reasoning model. We demonstrate that our proposed approach results in correctly flagging approximately 20\% of errors made by our state of the art image-based deep neural network disease classifier.

Medical Imaging and Diagnostic Ontology

Ontology has been cleared for release and is made available as an open-source resource under the Apache 2.0 license

We have designed an explanation ontology that captures the aspects of explanations related to their generation from a system's perspective while accounting for factors from an end-user's perspective. Through our modeling of the components involved in the process of explanation generation, we provide a means for system designers to translate their user requirements gathered from user studies to explanations that can be generated by their systems. We depict how our ontology schema can support the modeling of different literature-derived explanation types that help address the diverse needs of user-centric explainability.
